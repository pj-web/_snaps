The slow and painful demise of an industry we still love and cherish
The world’s second largest cinema chain may be preparing to file for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in the US and it kinda sucks. I mean, going to the movies is great – who’s gonna get the same experience watching Top Gun on their phone? Yet the facts remain: people are lazy and streaming is easy, plus Hollywood hasn’t exactly covered itself in glory with its recent slate of releases (Eternals, anyone?). So is it any wonder CINE stock is down nearly 90% this year, with $4.9bn in debt to just a ~$70M market cap. Crazy, but enough of the previews – onto the show.
Cineworld Group
A worrying preview for cinema stocks
Remember the days when you’d grab five of your closest pals, stock up on slurpees (with a sneaky dash of voddies mixed in perhaps) and hit the movies? Well, appaz those days are over.
Cineworld has confirmed it’s considering filing for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in the US as the world’s second largest cinema chain struggles to attract audiences back to the big screen. The chain is sitting with just under $5bn in debt, prompting discussions about restructuring the business and how they can find more cash.
Audience numbers remain far below expectations despite lockdown restrictions having lifted a good while ago, tho Cineworld reckons the lack of attendance isn’t bc people don’t like to the movies anymore, but more bc Hollywood is just making bad movies these days – ok ok, they used the words “limited film slate”, but that was the gist.
Its UK-listed stock plunged 58% on Friday to take its weekly losses to over 80%, leaving prices at a brand new all-time low even tho bankruptcy is only “one option” they’re considering to increase liquidity – if they do manage to find a better way to do that, it’ll be the third time in just over two years that the company has skirted bankruptcy.

BTC/USD
Crypto bulls get cold feet
Any crypto bulls that were taking off their wooly hats to prepare for the end of the crypto winter get a stark reminder that bear season ain’t over yet. Bundle up peeps.
The crypto industry felt a sharp selloff last week with nearly $200bn worth of crypto dumped. The value of the overall industry’s now fallen below $1tn – its lowest valuation since December 2020. BTC fell by nearly 12% last week with ETH taking an even bigger beating of 16% over the same period to close at its lowest price since mid-July.
The Fed’s monetary policy seems to be to blame as investors predict further rate-hikes incoming, which is prolly why crypto-related stocks also felt the squeeze. Coinbase plummeted by over 11% on Friday and famed HODLer MicroStrategy sank nearly 13% to erase its August gains.
Many analysts are warning of more turbulence ahead, pointing to the absence of a BTC futures premium, $470m in liquidations and unrestrained stablecoin lending as symptomatic of new lows to come. Worth noting that some tokens actually managed to defy the selloff entirely, with the likes of layer-1 blockchain EOS rallying more than 23% over the weekend while most others sold off.

Occidental Petroleum
Another Berkshire boost
Big dog investor Warren Buffett’s love affair with Occidental Petroleum is seriously heating up as the billionaire gets permission to go all-in on the energy giant.
Berkshire Hathaway has won approval to buy up to a 50% stake in his fave energy behemoth as the investor continues his massive bet on the oil industry, which is looking increasingly attractive as a natural hedge against soaring inflation. Berkshire already owned 188.5m shares, equal to around 20.2% of the company, having been lured in by its growth trajectory and leadership.
Is this the start of a full-blown takeover? Well, not just yet, but analysts think it would make “nothing but sense” for the relationship to head in that direction – given Occidental shares popped 10% on Friday for their best day since early March, it seems investors are chuffed about the flourishing romance and could be onboard with such a commitment.
2022 has already been a blowout year for Occidental investors, almost entirely thanks to oil’s bullish price action this year, and has seen shares soar 145% YTD. All that momentum has taken OXY to a key inflection point that’s allowed it to pay off more debt and start paying out more cash to investors through share repurchases and a rising dividend.

FTT/USD
Always read your insurance policy
The FDIC politely informs certain crypto companies that they might just have misunderstood the meaning of ‘FDIC insured’, and not-so-politely tells them to sort it out ASAP rocky.
The FDIC has issued cease-and-desist letters to five crypto companies including popular exchange FTX over misrepresentations of deposit insurance, getting given only 15 days to confirm that it has taken the appropriate action to address the “misunderstanding”. The other named companies included Cryptonews.com, FDICCrypto.com and Cryptosec.info.
They’ve been accused of claiming certain cryptos were ‘FDIC insured’, which means investors would get their money back if the token collapsed – which is not the case as it turns out. The agency’s recently been slapping the wrist of a slew of crypto companies and had to issue a statement last month saying that this kind of company is not insured, in light of Voyager’s bankruptcy filing.
FTX has had a highly successful past few years – taking revenue from below $90m in 2020 to over $1bn during 2021’s crypto boom. Regulatory hurdles don’t seem to have fazed Sam Bankman-Fried, the company’s entrepreneurial CEO, who’s been overseeing expansion by recently purchasing startups in Switzerland and Australia.

Foot Locker
Foot Locker steps up
Investors are feeling footloose and fancy free after better-than-expected earnings and the arrival of a new CEO puts a spring in their step.
Shares in Foot Locker popped over 20% on Friday to mark their best day of the year, taking prices to a six-month high and capping off their seventh straight week in the green. It comes after the brand posted EPS of $1.10 – which was down 50% YoY but still beat estimates – on revenues that were down 9% but just shy of expectations at $2.07bn.
Taking the spotlight was news of CEO Richard Johnson’s retirement on September 1, to be replaced by former Ulta Beauty exec and e-commerce guru Mary Dillon. Her expertise is significant as Foot Locker tries to pivot away from its traditional bricks and mortar model towards more of a digitally-focused strategy.
Dillon was the driving force behind Ulta’s turnaround story, and investors and execs alike are hoping she can perform that same magic on Foot Locker, which (despite the earnings beat) has been flailing amid supply chain disruptions, a pullback in business from Nike, and a decline in in-store sales. Dillon’s got her work cut out for her, but analysts reckon she’s “the best announcement that you can make”.
