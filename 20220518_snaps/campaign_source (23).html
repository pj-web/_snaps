The End of McHistory
After the Berlin Wall fell, Francis Fukuyama boldly proclaimed “the end of history” – appaz Western democracy had been decided as the final form of human government. The icon of that historic conclusion? McDonald’s. Indeed, when the golden arches opened its doors on Pushkin Square in 1990, a queue spanning several miles grew across Moscow, all to order Big Macs. But Fukuyama was wrong, history is all Jeremy Bearimy and now – 32 years later – Mickey Ds is leaving Russia. Let’s dig into the deets.
McDonald's
See overview
E-I-E-uh-oh
After more than three decades, the golden arches of McDonald’s are leaving Russia for good in light of “the humanitarian crisis in Ukraine”.
McDonald’s has made the decision to pull out of Russia permanently, three months after halting all operations in the region (along with a bunch of other consumer companies) amid the invasion of Ukraine in March. The company will sell off all of its 850 locations to a local buyer, who will also take care of its 62,000 employees.
The brand will record a write-off of up to $1.4bn due to “foreign currency translation losses” – hardly a surprise considering the Russian ruble has been on quite the rollercoaster ride recently. However, investors took the hit in their stride with prices remaining fairly stable, made easier by the fact that revenue losses will be light.
Old MacDonald has been facing increasing pressure from investors, consumers, and employees alike to exit Russia. The golden arches have long been a symbol of globalism and capitalism in the region, first appearing in 1990 as Cold War tensions thawed, and their departure is just as significant.

Snakes on a plane
Hostile takeover attempts are running rampant on Wall Street (thanks Elon), with JetBlue now taking aim at Spirit Airlines.
Spirit flew over 13.5% on Monday after hitting a more than 18-month low last week amid a broader market crash, following news that JetBlue is planning to attempt a hostile takeover of the budget airline.
It comes after Spirit rejected JetBlue’s $3.6bn takeover offer in favor of a lower offer from Frontier Airlines, citing antitrust concerns. It’s all part of a race to consolidation that’s seeing budget airlines try to join forces to take on the “Big Four” legacy airlines that rn control 80% of the US passenger market.
JetBlue offered Spirit investors $30 per share as a part of its tender offer, sending a proxy statement urging them to reject the Frontier deal when they take a vote at their June shareholder meeting. It argued the regulatory concerns are a “smokescreen to distract from the fact that its merger with Frontier faces similar regulatory risk” – stay tuned.

Is Bitcoin now the crypto grandpa?
A record-breaking seven straight weeks in the red, and now a Man from the Bank is saying Bitcoin has no place as a payment system? Ouch.
FTX CEO Sam Bankman-Fried voiced his doubts over Bitcoin’s scalability this week, claiming the proof-of-work (PoW) model can’t carry the weight of millions of transactions like proof-of-stake (PoS) can. To clear up the beef, he later tweeted he sees no real use-case for the OG crypto besides its store of asset value as digital gold.
Unsurprisingly, the Bitcoin maxis weren’t too pleased, and many in the space (like Jack Dorsey) stressed that he must distinguish between Bitcoin as a layer 1 network and its layer 2 solutions. Bitcoin can actually process a helluva lot of transactions, but only through the L2 solution Lightning Network has built on the blockchain.
Is Bitcoin falling out with the times? Its current seven-week downtrend doesn’t look great, we must admit. While Bitcoin has survived pretty much everything that’s been thrown at it, the growing dominance of PoS cryptos might scare off the HODLers – especially with Ethereum’s upcoming change of allegiance.

BuzzFeed fails its Q1 quiz
Turns out, there’s only so many times people wanna find out what type of Subway sandwich they are, and BuzzFeed’s balance sheet is struggling with the lower engagement.
Shares sank over 10% in Monday’s extended trading after the media conglomerate released a decidedly mixed bag for Q1, missing estimates by reporting a loss of $0.33 per share on revenues that grew 26% to hit $91.6m. BuzzFeed’s last earnings reported similar losses and announced that it’ll be shrinking its newsroom to compensate.
That revenue growth may look prettier than it is though – almost all of it is attributable to its acquisition of Complex Networks last December. In contrast, BuzzFeed’s commerce revenue was down 27% at $10.6m and its net losses ballooned on the back of supply chain delays, and that’s not all…
People are spending less and less time on the site, a metric that can’t really be blamed on macroeconomic conditions. User engagement declined by 4% from last quarter, though BuzzFeed tried to ease the pain by saying that doesn’t include its short form videos that make the rounds on other socials like TikTok. Only time will tell if that’s true.

Deus Finance gets the depegging treatment
Next up in Depeg Roulette is stablecoin DEI, which runs on Fantom’s DeFi network Deus Finance and is falling waaay off its dollar peg.
DEI fell as low as $0.52 on some exchanges on Monday, and is still trading for only $0.61 as of Tuesday morning, coming at a time when algorithmic stablecoins face a barrage of… well, stability concerns. Deus Finance reckons the moment of weakness was bc of people swapping their DEI tokens for USDC without enough liquidity to fill the exodus.
The stablecoin is a part of DeFi protocol DEUS Finance, and is used primarily as collateral. Like UST, DEI works on an algorithmic mechanism that trades DEI with $1 worth of DEUS tokens to keep its dollar peg through artificially controlling supply and demand.
Calm your horses, this is unlikely to impact the wider market. DEI only has a market capitalization of $60m and Deus Finance claims it's found a repegging solution through a treasury bond program… whatever that’s supposed to mean. As of writing, however, it’s still not met its parity – so get those bonds out, DEI.
